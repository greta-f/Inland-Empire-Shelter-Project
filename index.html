🐾 California Animal Shelter Crisis: Inland Empire 🐾

California’s animal shelter system is facing a growing crisis, driven by rising intake numbers, limited capacity, and uneven resource distribution across regions. In recent years, shelters have seen an increase in owner surrenders, stray populations, and longer animal stays, all while staffing, funding, and space have struggled to keep pace. As a result, many facilities are operating beyond their intended capacity, forcing difficult decisions about care, placement, and outcomes. This strain is not evenly distributed—some regions are experiencing far more severe overcrowding and resource shortages than others. This strain is very apparent in the Inland Empire, particularly in Riverside and San Bernardino Counties. These counties cover vast geographic areas—San Bernardino alone is the largest county in the United States—yet shelter infrastructure has not scaled proportionally with population growth or regional needs. High intake numbers, combined with limited shelter density and long distances between facilities, create significant challenges for both animals and the communities they serve. The result is a system under pressure, where shelters are often operating at or beyond capacity, and where access to services can vary widely depending on location. This project explores those spatial disparities, highlighting how geography, scale, and resource distribution contribute to a system that is increasingly approaching its limits.
