Exploring the Growing Strain on Inland Empire Animal Shelters

California’s animal shelter system is facing a growing crisis driven by rising intake numbers, limited capacity, and uneven resource distribution across regions. In recent years, shelters have seen an increase in owner surrenders, stray populations, and longer animal stays, all while staffing, funding, and space have struggled to keep pace. As a result, many facilities are operating beyond their intended capacity, forcing difficult decisions about care, placement, and outcomes. This strain is not evenly distributed—some regions are experiencing far more severe overcrowding and resource shortages than others.

Nowhere is this crisis more apparent than in Southern California, particularly in Los Angeles and the Inland Empire—Riverside and San Bernardino Counties. These counties cover vast geographic areas—San Bernardino alone is the largest county in the contiguous United States—yet shelter infrastructure has not scaled proportionally with population growth or regional needs. High intake numbers, combined with limited shelter density and long distances between facilities, create significant challenges for both animals and the communities they serve. The result is a system under pressure, where shelters are often operating at or beyond capacity, and where access to services can vary widely depending on location. This project explores those spatial disparities, highlighting how geography, scale, and resource distribution contribute to a system that is increasingly approaching its limits. This site explores the animal shelter crisis with a focus on the Inland Empire: Riverside and San Bernardino Counties.

How You Can Help

Addressing the animal shelter crisis requires community involvement at every level. Individuals can play a meaningful role in reducing overcrowding and improving outcomes for animals through several key actions.



  • Adopt: Choosing to adopt rather than purchase pets directly reduces shelter populations and gives animals a second chance at a permanent home.

  • Foster: Temporary fostering helps free up space in shelters, reduces stress for animals, and increases their chances of adoption.

  • Volunteer: Shelters rely heavily on volunteers for daily operations, including animal care, enrichment, and administrative support.

  • Transport: Volunteer transport programs for both rescues and shelters help move animals from overcrowded shelters to facilities with more resources or higher adoption demand, or to rescues.

  • Spay and Neuter: Preventing unplanned litters is one of the most effective ways to reduce shelter intakee, euthanization and control overpopulation.

  • Networking: Networkers on social media platforms ensure the animals in the shelter are given a chance to be seen by potential adopters and rescue groups.

These actions not only support individual animals but also contribute to broader efforts to stabilize shelter systems and improve animal welfare across the region.
