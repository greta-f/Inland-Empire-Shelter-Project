Shelter Distribution and County Scale

This is one of two maps that illustrate the distribution of animal shelters across Riverside and San Bernardino Counties. In this map you can see the shelter locationd in relation to county boundaries and service areas. By displaying individual shelter locations, the map highlights how facilities are concentrated in more urbanized areas, while large portions of the region—particularly in eastern San Bernardino County—have few or no nearby shelters.

The scale of these counties is significant. San Bernardino County, the largest county in the contiguous United States, covers a vast geographic area, making access to shelter services more challenging for residents in remote and desert communities. This spatial imbalance emphasizes the importance of geographic scale when evaluating service accessibility in the Inland Empire.

In more remote and desert regions, additional factors contribute to elevated intake levels. Animal abandonment—including the dumping of pets in isolated areas—places increased strain on the limited number of nearby shelters, which already serve expansive geographic regions. These patterns intensify existing capacity challenges and highlight the uneven distribution of both resources and demand across the Inland Empire. Furthermore, the rural location of many shelters often results in fewer available volunteers, compounding the operational difficulties these facilities face. As shelters reach capacity, many are forced to turn away animals brought in by the public, shifting the burden of care onto surrounding communities.
Although Inland Valley Humane Society is located in Pomona within Los Angeles County, its geographic proximity to the Inland Empire allows it to serve populations in both Riverside and San Bernardino Counties, highlighting the importance of cross-county resource networks.

Why Aren't Rescues Displayed?

Rescues play a vital role in saving animals from shelters. In addition to providing medical care and behavioral training, rescues offer animals a second chance by placing them in environments where they can be better observed outside the stress of a shelter setting. Shelter conditions can often mask an animal’s true personality, making adoption more difficult.

Unlike municipal shelters, rescues typically do not operate within strict geographic boundaries and are able to “pull” animals from any shelter with which they are approved to partner. This network can extend beyond state lines, as out-of-state rescues may also transfer animals provided they are registered 501(c)(3) organizations and meet required agreements, such as ensuring spay/neuter procedures and vaccinations within a specified timeframe.
